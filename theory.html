Definition 1

Adaptive Efficiency (AE)

Adaptive Efficiency is defined as the instantaneous rate of change of the Capability Function C(t) with respect to time. Unlike thermodynamic efficiency (bounded by Carnot) or standard AI performance metrics (presupposing a fixed hypothesis class H), AE is defined over systems where H itself evolves.

Adaptive Efficiency measures how fast a system is improving itself — not how well it does on a fixed test, but how rapidly it is growing its ability to solve harder problems over time. Think of it like measuring how fast you're getting smarter, not just how smart you are right now.

Imagine a video game character that doesn't just level up in strength — it actually rewrites its own abilities. Adaptive Efficiency measures how fast it's rewriting. A character with high AE is basically becoming a different, better game every few minutes.

Definition 1.1 — Adaptive Efficiency
AE(t) = dC(t) / dt
Where C(t) is the Capability Function at time t

Definition 2

The Capability Function C(t)

C(t) is a weighted composite of cognitive and physical capability components, measured against a dynamic, evolving benchmark suite B(t). The dynamic benchmark is critical: a system that memorizes a fixed test achieves high apparent performance without positive AE in the meaningful sense.

The Capability Function is like a combined score that measures two things: how well the agent thinks (cognitive tasks like coding and reasoning) and how well it acts in the physical world. These are blended together with weights α and β that add up to 1.

Think of C(t) as your total game score — part brain score, part physical score. As you get better, the game automatically makes harder levels (dynamic benchmark) so you can't just cheat by memorizing the old levels.

Definition 2.1 — Capability Function
C(t) = α · C_cog(t) + β · C_phys(t)
α + β = 1, α = 0.7, β = 0.3 (paper baseline)

Model

Stochastic Capability Dynamics

The physically realistic model extends the deterministic exponential with four factors: stochastic noise ξ(t), a logistic resource ceiling C_max, a failure probability p_fail, and the Kolmogorov incompressibility correction δ_K(t). This replaces the naive exponential with a physically grounded growth law analogous to the Verhulst equation.

The real simulation is messier than a simple growth curve. Sometimes modifications fail. Sometimes there's random noise. There's a ceiling on how much the machine can grow (limited computer resources). And as the machine gets more complex, improvements get harder to find. All four effects are baked into one equation.

Imagine leveling up, but sometimes you make a mistake and lose a little XP (p_fail), sometimes random stuff happens (noise σ), eventually you hit a max level (C_max), and later in the game each level takes longer to reach (δ_K). That's this equation.

Stochastic Update Equation
C(t+1) = C(t) + F(t)·[λ_eff·C(t)·(1 − C(t)/C_max) − δ_K(t)] + σ·C(t)·ξ(t)
ξ(t) ~ N(0,1) · F(t) ~ Bernoulli(1 − p_fail) · λ_eff = α·λ_cog + β·λ_phys
λ_eff·C(t)·(1−C/C_max) — logistic growth term (bounded by resources)
−δ_K(t) — Kolmogorov incompressibility correction (grows with complexity)
F(t) — Bernoulli failure variable (zero on failed modifications)
σ·C(t)·ξ(t) — multiplicative noise (proportional to current capability)
Theorem

The Adaptive Efficiency Growth Theorem

THEOREM (Adaptive Efficiency Growth)

Let a Self-Evolving Machine operate under the stochastic model above. Define μ_fail as the expected fractional capability loss per failure event.

NECESSARY CONDITION for E[AE(t)] > 0
λ_eff · C(t) · (1 − C(t)/C_max) > δ_K(t) + p_fail · μ_fail · C(t)
SUFFICIENT CONDITION for sustained E[AE(t)] > 0 over [0, T]
λ_eff > (σ² / 2) + p_fail · μ_fail + δ_K_max / C_0
Failure Regime FR-1

Noise Dominance

σ²/2 > λ_eff − p_fail·μ_fail

Analogous to the order-disorder phase transition in spin glasses. Below a critical signal-to-noise ratio, no ordered (improving) state is stable. The stochastic fluctuations overwhelm the systematic improvement signal — AE fluctuates around zero with no sustained trend.

When there's too much randomness in the evaluation signal, the machine can't tell if a modification is actually good or just got lucky. It's like trying to tune a radio station in a thunderstorm — the signal gets drowned by noise.

Too much random stuff happening. The machine makes a change, but it can't tell if it helped because everything is too noisy. Like trying to judge if a study session helped when you also had 3 coffees, slept 2 hours, and the exam was weirdly hard.

Physical analog: Order-disorder phase transition in spin glasses
Failure Regime FR-2

Resource Saturation

C(t) → C_max ⟹ AE(t) → −δ_K(t) < 0

The logistic growth ceiling: as C(t) approaches C_max, the improvement gradient shrinks to zero while the incompressibility correction δ_K(t) continues to grow, driving AE negative. Fundamentally a resource economics problem — increasing C_max delays but does not eliminate the regime.

The machine eventually hits the limits of its available computing power. There's only so much RAM, only so many GPU cycles. As it approaches that ceiling, improvements get smaller and smaller — like trying to go faster in a car that's already at its top speed.

The machine maxes out its computing power. Like a phone at 100% storage — you can't install new apps no matter how good they are. The fix isn't to try harder; it's to get a bigger phone (more compute) or find smarter ways to organize what you have.

Physical analog: Carrying capacity limit in ecological thermodynamics (logistic equation)
Failure Regime FR-3

Algorithmic Incompressibility

δ_K(t) ≥ λ_eff · C(t) · (1 − C(t)/C_max)

The deepest failure mode, grounded in Chaitin-Kolmogorov theory. When K(A_t) — the Kolmogorov complexity of the agent description — increases faster than the modification strategy can discover improvements, AE falls. No amount of additional compute escapes this regime; what's required is a qualitative innovation in the modification strategy — second-order self-improvement.

As the machine gets smarter, describing each improvement requires more and more information. Eventually the cost of specifying a better version of itself outpaces its ability to find one. It's like writing a program to improve a program — but the improvement instructions are longer than the program itself.

The machine has become so complex that finding the next upgrade is nearly impossible — like trying to explain an advanced calculus concept in only 10 words. The information needed to describe the improvement is bigger than the improvement itself. This is the deepest, most fundamental wall.

Physical analog: Chaitin incompressibility limit in algorithmic information theory
Physical Foundation

Why This Is a Physics Problem

The connection between AI self-improvement and physics is not metaphorical. Three independent bodies of physics jointly constrain and explain SEM behavior.

Landauer 1961

Landauer's Principle

Minimum erasure energy
E_erase ≥ k_B · T · ln(2) ≈ 2.87 × 10⁻²¹ J
At T = 300K (room temperature)

Every self-modification cycle erases information (failed modification candidates). Landauer's principle sets a physical floor on the energy cost of every such cycle. The DGM archive strategy — retaining rather than erasing failed modifications — is thermodynamically optimal: it avoids the erasure cost while preserving the information value of suboptimal variants.

Prigogine 1977 · Nobel Prize

Dissipative Structures

Prigogine's theory identifies open physical systems that maintain and increase internal organization by consuming free energy and exporting entropy. The SEM is a dissipative structure in this precise technical sense: it imports computational energy and exports entropy through discarded modifications. Importantly, removing the energy supply causes collapse to thermodynamic equilibrium — making resource constraints thermodynamic stability conditions, not merely engineering considerations.

Kolmogorov Complexity

The Incompressibility Ceiling

Kolmogorov complexity bound
K(A_t) = length of shortest program producing A_t
No program produces A_t with fewer bits — this bound is absolute

As the agent becomes more capable, K(A_t) increases monotonically. Self-modifications that further increase complexity require progressively longer programs to specify — making them increasingly hard to discover through search. Sustained positive AE therefore requires not just first-order self-modification but second-order improvement of the modification strategy itself.

System Design

Architecture Overview

The SEM consists of six functional layers forming a closed loop. Each layer's function can be derived directly from the theorem: the AE evaluator implements the necessary condition check; the safety monitor enforces the sufficient condition; the archive implements Landauer-optimal retention.
